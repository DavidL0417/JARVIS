             Timeline
             First Thought — Middle school. Yoyo was naturally organized and was drawn to physical
             planners and notebooks while shopping for school supplies. This was a self-driven extension of
             her journaling habit, not something her parents pushed. The instinct that her life needed
             structure came from her, very early.
             Passive Looking — Early high school. Physical planners started becoming a friction point. She
             would forget to bring the planner from her room, and carrying paper alongside laptop and iPad
             felt redundant. She still preferred paper, but the inconvenience accumulated. Extracurriculars
             and college-application pressure added complexity, and she began noticing she needed a better
             system without yet acting on it.
             Event #1 — Workload tipping point (freshman year of HS) — Sitting in her room or the library,
             the pressure of homework and college-prep work compounded until she finally opened the App
             Store and Google to look for a digital task manager. There was no single dramatic moment; the
             load simply crossed a threshold. Therefore, this is what flipped her from passively tolerating
             paper friction to actively shopping for software.
             Active Looking → Todoist (freshman HS) — Searched the App Store, picked Todoist, and paid
             for premium. It worked for the basic to-do function, however she found it underpowered: she
             wanted calendar integration, file linking, richer categorization, and more interactive features. She
             would have stayed on Todoist if nothing better had appeared.
             Event #2 — Notion trend plus roommate influence (sophomore HS) — Notion entered the
             cultural moment, and her roommate became obsessed with it. The combination of a trusted
             close-proximity user and the aesthetic appeal of databases-plus-notes made Notion feel
             revolutionary. Consequently, this is what flipped her from "Todoist is fine" to "I have to try this."
             Deciding and Buying (Notion, sophomore HS) — Adopted Notion, spent significant time
             customizing it and making it look good, and eventually paid because student free accounts were
             not available and her database load had outgrown the free tier. For a while, this felt like the
             answer.
             Event #3 — Notion fatigue plus Motion ad (senior HS / early freshman year of college) — Two
             things happened in parallel. Notion's setup cost began feeling punishing every time her life
             structure shifted (high school to college, summer to school year), and her customization habit
             turned into a time sink rather than a productivity gain. Around the same time, an Instagram ad
             surfaced Motion with the promise of AI-generated daily schedules. The pull of "no more manual
             restructuring" combined with the push of Notion fatigue moved her to try something new.
             Active Looking → Motion (free trial) — Used Motion during the free trial. The AI scheduling was
             not accurate enough, and more importantly, she did not want to surrender control of her day to
             an agent. Therefore, she did not convert to paid.
