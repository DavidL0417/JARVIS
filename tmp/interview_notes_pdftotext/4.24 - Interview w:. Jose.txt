             Timeline
             First Thought — High school. The school handed out free planners every year, which planted the
             idea that a planner was a normal thing to own. Jose would use it for the first 2–3 weeks of each
             school year and then drop it, leaning on Google Classroom instead. So the concept was familiar,
             but never stuck as a real habit.
             Passive Looking — Around July, before college, Jose was making a list of school supplies
             (notebooks, markers, highlighters, pens, pencils, stapler). A planner crossed his mind and he
             thought about adding it, but never actually wrote it on the list. Idea acknowledged, not committed
             to.
             Event #1 — Sister at the store — At Staples with his sister, the planner came up again. She
             pushed him to get one, arguing that a physical planner would actually be useful in college even
             though it hadn't worked in high school. Both his sister and his mom use physical planners
             regularly, which framed it as "a college thing to do." Combined with his own desire to "lock in"
             and get his shit together, this is what flipped him from passively considering to actively looking
             on the spot.
             Active Looking — Started going through the planners on the shelf. The bar was simple: it had to
             look good and be in good condition. Most were beat up — deteriorated synthetic leather covers,
             bent and twisted rings — and he didn't want to spend money on something already damaged.
             Event #2 — The bad-condition shelf — Realizing almost every planner in the stack was bent up
             or scuffed pushed him from "grab one" to "I need to actually find one worth my money." The
             visual/condition issue, not price or features, became the deciding constraint.
             Deciding — Spent about five minutes digging through the stack. The plan was: find an
             acceptable one in store, otherwise buy one online later. Near the bottom of the pile he found one
             in good enough shape, and the decision became quick and almost mindless — straight into the
             cart.
             Buying — Purchased the planner at Staples along with the rest of his supplies.
             Consuming — None. Didn't use it during the Bridge program as he'd intended. Brought it to
             Northwestern and carried it around for a while planning to "use it eventually," then pulled it out of
             his backpack to make room for a textbook.
             Satisfaction — None. Realized about a month ago that he had never actually used it. Same
             pattern as high school: bought into the idea, never built the habit.
