First Thought — High school​
 In high school, he relied mostly on memory and Google Classroom to keep track of
assignments. Occasionally, he would try using planners at the start of the school year, but
would abandon them within 2–3 weeks. The idea of planning and scheduling existed, but it
never translated into a sustained habit. His system was reactive, but it worked “well enough”
in a lower-pressure environment.



Passive Looking — Early college (pre-finals)​
After arriving at Duke, he became loosely aware that college required better time
management. There was a background thought that he needed to be more organized, but no
urgency. He continued relying on memory, Canvas, and last-minute effort. He was exposed
to productivity tools like Notion through social media and peers, but did not immediately act
on it.



Event #1 — Poor performance on final​
 During his first finals week, he had an economics exam that he had known about well in
advance. Despite this, he repeatedly delayed studying, prioritizing more immediate or easier
tasks. He ended up cramming the material into approximately a day and a half. During the
exam, he realized he was missing concepts he could have mastered with better planning.
This created a strong sense of regret because the failure felt avoidable and self-inflicted.



Active Looking — Post-finals realization​
 Following the exam, he recognized that his reactive system was insufficient for college. The
failure created urgency: he needed a structured system to manage his time. Around this
period, he also noticed peers appearing more organized and saw multiple examples of
Notion dashboards online. His roommate already had a system in place, reinforcing the idea
that he was behind.



Event #2 — Social comparison + identity pressure​
 Seeing peers and online examples of “organized students” created pressure to adopt a
similar system. Organization became tied not just to productivity, but identity — being the
type of student who has their life together. This pushed him from passive awareness into
actively trying a solution.



Deciding — Trying Notion (Free → Premium)​
He downloaded Notion and began exploring templates. His decision criteria were vague —
primarily aesthetic and the feeling of control. He used the free version for several weeks,
during which he actively engaged with the tool and experimented with setups. Because the
initial experience felt promising and aligned with his goals, he upgraded to the premium
version, expecting long-term usage.



Consuming — Initial success, then drop-off​
 In the first 1–2 weeks, he consistently used Notion to track tasks, deadlines, and plan his
days. It created a sense of control and organization. However, maintaining the system
required constant manual input and updates. Missing even a single day led to inconsistency,
and over time, he stopped checking and updating it. The system gradually became
irrelevant.



Satisfaction — Low (Idea good, execution failed)​
 He ultimately stopped using Notion and returned to a reactive system (memory, calendar,
Canvas). He recognized that while the idea of structured planning was appealing, the tool
required a level of discipline and consistency he did not yet have. The failure was not due to
lack of understanding, but due to a mismatch between the tool’s requirements and his
behavior.




4 Forces of Progress

Push (of the present)
   ●​   Poor performance on a final due to last-minute cramming
   ●​   Realization that failure was avoidable and caused by poor time allocation
   ●​   Transition to a more demanding college environment where tasks overlap
   ●​   Awareness that his high school system (memory + reaction) no longer works




Pull (of the new solution)
   ●​   Exposure to Notion dashboards and productivity content online
   ●​   Social comparison with peers who appeared more organized
   ●​   Roommate already having a structured system in place
   ●​   Desire to feel in control and “on top of things”
   ●​   Appeal of a clean, aesthetic, structured interface
Habit (of the present) — “What I do now isn’t that bad”
  ●​   Reliance on Canvas, calendar, and memory for tracking tasks
  ●​   Pattern of short-term planner usage followed by abandonment
  ●​   Comfort with reactive workflows (responding to urgency rather than planning ahead)
  ●​   Lack of a deeply ingrained planning habit




Anxiety (of the new solution) — “Will this actually
work?”
  ●​   Concern about maintaining consistency over time
  ●​   Uncertainty about how to integrate the tool into daily routines
  ●​   Effort required to manually input and update tasks
  ●​   Fear that the system will be abandoned like previous planners
  ●​   Doubt about whether the tool fits his natural workflow
