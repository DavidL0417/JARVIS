             First Thought — COVID, middle school. People around her were getting sick and dying. That
             pushed her toward wanting to help in a future crisis through medicine, pharmacy, or research. To
             get there she had to get into a good university, which meant becoming a serious student. This
             ambition created a need for better productivity.
             Passive Looking — Her primary goal was to "be a better student so I can help people." She
             wasn't shopping for productivity tools, but she was just generally aware that her phone and
             social media kept pulling her off schoolwork and that she needed to study more seriously.
             Event #1 — Social media surfaced an app for her — Productivity-app content showed up in her
             feed and she clicked through to download her first one, 嘀嗒清单 (free version), around 7th-8th
             grade. She found the app through her feed rather than active searching. This prompted her to
             start trying productivity tools.
             Active Looking — She actively tested various apps to help her become a better student. The
             pattern: see a productivity app on social media, download it immediately, use it briefly, uninstall
             because something was always wrong. 嘀嗒清单 free capped her labels so she could only mark
             generic "productive time" instead of separating English from math. Forest left a dead tree on the
             screen forever if a session was interrupted, which broke the whole point of the app for her. Each
             one had a specific dealbreaker.
             Event #2 — Focus Pomo came through the same channel — Around 10th grade, Focus Pomo
             appeared on her social media feed the same way every other app had. She downloaded it and
             used the free version for about a weekend.
             Deciding — free vs. paid, and what was finally not wrong — She evaluated Focus Pomo based
             on whether it avoided the specific dealbreakers of her previous apps. It met this criteria. 嘀嗒清
             单's label cap was solved by Focus Pomo's premium tier, which allowed unlimited categories and
             gave her percentage breakdowns by subject in the recap. Forest's dead-tree failure mode had no
             equivalent here — sessions resolved cleanly with a tomato. App-blocking and cross-device sync
             were both present. The free Focus Pomo tier still capped her at three categories, which was the
             same problem she had hit before, so the choice was effectively paid or move on.
             Buying — By 10th grade her allowance was larger and the price felt fair for unlimited labels and
             the recap view. She had already tested the workflow on the free version over the weekend, so
             the upgrade carried very little risk. She paid.
             Satisfaction and Usage — Still actively using it as a UCLA first-year. The only app from years of
             cycling that has actually stuck.
