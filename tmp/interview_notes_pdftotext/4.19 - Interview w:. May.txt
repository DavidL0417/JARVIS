             Timeline
             First Thought — Start of junior year (late 2022 / early 2023). SAT prep, homework, college
             counselor meetings, and club activities all started stacking up. She began sensing her laptop
             notes weren't cutting it. Freshman and sophomore year had been light enough that she'd never
             needed a real system.
             Passive Looking — For about three weeks, she kept using her laptop notes. Nothing was actively
             broken — she was still getting her work done. She vaguely noticed it looked messy and ugly, but
             wasn't researching alternatives yet. Junior-year workload kept building quietly in the
             background.
             Event #1 — The desk moment — One February afternoon, sitting at her desk in her George
             School dorm with her laptop open, trying to schedule when to do each assignment. Nothing
             external changed that day — no missed deadline, no crisis, her system still worked. She simply
             decided she couldn't keep working off ugly notes and needed to actually go find something
             better. This is what flipped her from tolerating to searching.
             Active Looking — Searched Red Note (小红书) first, but the results felt like ads and weren't
             useful. Then asked a friend what she actually used, and her friend recommended TickTick.
             Event #2 — Friend's recommendation landed — When her friend named TickTick and
             specifically called out the calendar integration pulling in Google + Outlook, she suddenly had a
             concrete, trusted option in hand. No reason to keep shopping. This is what flipped her from "I
             should get an app" to "I'm downloading this one, today."
             Deciding — Friend surfaced two options: TickTick and another simpler app (name forgotten).
             Picked TickTick because it had more varied functions — calendar integration, countdown,
             Pomodoro, etc.
             Buying — Downloaded TickTick the same day. Used the free version for about a week, then
             bought the annual premium subscription, mostly to unlock the calendar integration that pulls in
             Google Calendar and Outlook events.
             Consuming — Used it throughout 11th grade. Leaned on the unified calendar view, the
             countdown for deadline pressure, the Pomodoro timer (番茄钟), drag-and-drop scheduling, and
             the white noise feature.
             Satisfaction — Genuinely helpful. Gave her a comprehensive view of everything she had to do
             and pushed her productivity up. But she didn't renew after year one — the subscription felt too
             expensive to justify — and she hasn't used TickTick since.
