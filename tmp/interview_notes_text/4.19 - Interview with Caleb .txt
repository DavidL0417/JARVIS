Timeline, Forces Diagram — Caleb (Time Management App)  Timeline First thought — Mid Winter Quarter (~Week 4–5)  Caleb’s first exposure to the idea of using a structured productivity system came during a conversation with his friend Jaden, a highly productive student in a BS/MS program. They discussed how to manage multiple commitments while still being successful, and Jaden mentioned using a task management app, TickTick. At that point, the idea registered more as a possibility than a decision,  something along the lines of “this could help me be more productive,” but without immediate action. Passive looking — Weeks prior   Even before that conversation, Caleb was already aware of inefficiencies in his workflow. He described recurring patterns of procrastination, where assignments would be given weeks in advance but delayed until the last moment, forcing him into periods of intense cramming. Although he recognized that this approach was inefficient, his response remained informal, occasional conversations with his father or friends for motivation rather than actively seeking structured solutions. There was no deliberate search, no comparison of tools, and no concrete attempt to change behavior at this stage. Event 1 — Recurring inefficiency pattern  Over time, the repetition of this cycle became more salient: wasted hours early in the week would accumulate into pressure later on. Caleb began to realize that even though he did not immediately feel the consequences of procrastination, they inevitably surfaced closer to deadlines. This recognition created a growing dissatisfaction with his current habits, but still did not immediately translate into action. Active looking — After unproductive study session  The turning point occurred after a late-night study session at the library (Mudd), where Caleb felt unproductive despite having both time and energy available. This moment made the inefficiency of his current approach more concrete and immediate. Instead of passively acknowledging the issue, he moved into problem-solving mode and reached out to Jaden for advice, signaling a shift from awareness to action.

Event 2 — Peer advice (trigger moment)  During this call, Jaden emphasized the importance of being honest with oneself about productivity and continuing to work even when it feels uncomfortable. He also suggested small behavioral techniques, such as mentally resetting before starting work. Crucially, he recommended TickTick as a tool he had used. This moment served as the key trigger that connected Caleb’s problem to a specific solution, transforming abstract dissatisfaction into a clear next step. Deciding — Immediate, trust-based decision  Caleb’s decision-making process was notably fast. Rather than comparing multiple apps or evaluating alternatives, he relied entirely on Jaden’s recommendation. The decision was simply to try the one suggested to him.  Buying — Free trial adoption  He downloaded TickTick and began using the free trial version. The absence of an upfront financial commitment reduced the barrier to entry and made the transition into usage seamless. Adoption was immediate and practical. Consuming — End of Winter Quarter (~2–3 weeks)  During the final weeks of the winter quarter, Caleb actively used the app to organize tasks, track habits, and structure his workload. He found it more effective than Google Calendar, particularly because it allowed him to centralize all responsibilities in one place without needing multiple calendars. He also appreciated the categorization features, cleaner interface, and more targeted reminders, which felt less intrusive and more actionable. During this period, he experienced improved organization, reduced procrastination, and a greater sense of control over his time. However, his usage remained somewhat inconsistent, indicating that the tool had not yet become fully embedded in his daily routine. Satisfaction — Positive but incomplete adoption  Overall, Caleb’s experience with TickTick was positive. He reported feeling more organized and believed the app helped him manage his time more effectively during the period he used it. It gave him a sense of being “on the right track” and contributed to short-term behavioral improvements.

4 Forces Push — Pain with the current situation  Caleb’s adoption of the app was driven primarily by frustration with his existing habits. He experienced chronic procrastination, which led to last-minute cramming and inefficient use of time. Despite having available hours to work, he often failed to allocate them effectively, resulting in stress and underperformance. He also struggled with punctuality and felt a disconnect between the amount of work he was taking on and his ability to execute it successfully. Moments like the unproductive study session made these issues more tangible, reinforcing the need for a change. Pull — Attraction of the solution  The appeal of TickTick lay in its ability to centralize and simplify his workflow. Unlike Google Calendar, which required managing multiple calendars, the app offered a single platform where all tasks could be organized and categorized. Its cleaner interface and more intuitive design made it easier to use, while its reminders felt more intentional and actionable.  Habit — Inertia of old behavior  At the same time, Caleb’s existing habits worked against sustained adoption. He was accustomed to procrastination cycles and an unstructured approach to work, often filling his time with low-priority activities such as reorganizing or attending unnecessary meetings. These patterns were deeply ingrained, and his identity as a student was not yet fully aligned with being highly structured or disciplined. As a result, even after adopting the app, he struggled to use it consistently. Anxiety — Friction around the new solution  There were also several factors creating friction around continued use of the app. Caleb expressed doubts about his ability to maintain discipline over time, as well as a reluctance to pay for subscriptions, even when the cost was reasonable. He also questioned whether the app was necessary once he had developed better habits, and managing multiple systems (such as combining the app with Google Calendar) felt cumbersome.
